COURSE RATIONALE:
This course is timely and important as educators interrogate the needs of 21st century learners and how 21st century teachers will support these learning needs. Fundamental to addressing these needs is understanding the nature, value, and impact of curriculum, that is, how curriculum as a dynamic field both impacts and is impacted by societal changes. This understanding should better position third-year aspiring teachers to appreciate the pertinent role they play as stakeholders in the process of curriculum development, management, and evaluation. Trends in curriculum reform, the influence of globalization on education, and evidence of a mismatch between the written (formal) curriculum and contextual realities serve to confirm the need for teachers to acquire foundational knowledge of the field and how it operates. This implies that teachers at all levels of the education system should (a) possess the skills and dispositions for making curriculum-related decisions (b) keep pace with global trends and (c) be au-fait with the principles for designing, managing, and evaluating/assessing curricula that are contextually relevant.
COURSE DESCRIPTION:
This course introduces the foundations of curriculum and diverse theoretical and ideological frameworks for developing, implementing, managing, and evaluating curricula in various educational contexts. Aspiring teachers are engaged in authentic learning experiences that equip them to apply the technical skills for designing, developing, and evaluating curriculum as an integral part of their orientation to the teaching profession. The course is organized in three units:
  1. Unit 1: Foundations of the Curriculum
  2. Unit 2: Introduction to Curriculum Design and Development.
  3. Unit 3: Introduction to Curriculum Implementation and Management
The assessment tasks provide the opportunities for aspiring teachers to develop the competencies in designing and developing a curriculum. The course is assessed by coursework only and includes in-class activities, group projects and in-class tests. Aspiring teachers are engaged in research and critical discourse surrounding curricular concepts.
COURSE LEARNING OUTCOMES
At the end of the course, participants will:
  • Design a curriculum unit that appropriately addresses current educational issues.
  • Evaluate the professional disposition of curriculum specialists, while acknowledging their role as teachers who contribute to the curriculum process.
  • Reflect on the process of curriculum implementation as a change process.
COURSE CONTENT OUTLINE
UNIT 1: Foundations of the Curriculum
(18 Hours)
UNIT LEARNER OUTCOMES
  • Discuss various perspectives of curriculum.
  • Differentiate among the types of curricula.
  • Analyze the impact of curricular conceptions and foundations on educational initiatives.
  • Review the nature of the curriculum and its impact on teaching and learning.
SUMMARY OF CONTENT
  1. Define the perspectives of the curriculum.
  2. Types of curricula - hidden, null, formal, informal, concomitant etc.
  3. Conceptions of curriculum: Academic, Cognitive process, Humanistic, Technology, Social Reconstruction.
  4. Foundations of the curriculum:
    • Philosophical O Traditional and Contemporary.
    • Historical- Pre and Post Emancipation (Jamaica)
    • Sociology of Education
    • Psychological- impact of the field of psychology on education.
  5. Past and Present Educational Trends and Initiatives such as: ROSE, ASTEP, CAP (Jamaica), STEM/STEAM/STREAM, SWPBIS, Civics Education, Multicultural Education
SUGGESTED LEARNING ACTIVITIES
  • Critical discussion of how foundations influence curriculum design, development and implementation.
  • Debate on philosophical, sociological, and psychological issues.
  • Review features of curriculum documents/guides design.
  • Review initiatives (past and current) in light of how they have been influenced by these foundations.
FORMATIVE AND/OR DIAGNOSTIC ASSESSMENT
  • Reflections on curriculum concepts.
  • Writing a curriculum philosophical statement.

ACADEMIC CONDUCT POLICY
CLASS ATTENDANCE
Students are expected to arrive for class on time and to remain for the scheduled period for the class. A student is considered to be late 10 minutes after the start of class as scheduled on the timetable. If a student misses 30% or more of the scheduled class time, he/she is deemed to be absent.
ASSESSMENT
Students are responsible for meeting ALL assessment requirements for each course in which they are enrolled including deadlines established by the lecturer.
Late Submission
The penalty for late submission will be 5% of the total possible mark for the assessment per normal working day for up to 10 working days after the due date for submission.

A student will be required to sign, indicating that the assignment has been handed in and will be given a receipt. Keep the receipt in a safe place. No assignment will be accepted after 10 working days.
ACADEMIC CONDUCT
Students are expected to observe reasonable rules of conduct in classrooms and laboratories, and during all activities that are part of course requirements. The lecturer has the authority to deny the disruptive student the privilege of attending the class.
Plagiarism
Plagiarism includes, but is not limited to, the exact duplication of another's work and the incorporation of a substantial or essential portion without appropriate citation, copying and pasting information from websites or any publications, paying for a paper written by someone else or using one's work which was previously submitted for a grade in another course.
Consequences of Academic Misconduct
In all cases, a report will be prepared using the Academic Misconduct Report Form (AMRF). In cases of plagiarism, based on the severity of the offence, a faculty member may employ any of the following, in accordance with the Academic Integrity Policy:
  1. Warning: If the faculty member believes this to be the first offense, he/she indicates to the student that further academic dishonesty will result in other sanctions being imposed.
  2. Resubmission of work: The faculty member may require that the work in question be rewritten in conformance with proper academic standards and requirements. The faculty member may also specify other requirements.
  3. Grade reduction: The student's grade for a test, project or other academic work may be lowered.
  4. Failing: A grade of "E" is assigned for the assignment.
